\section{Ontology Engineering}
\label{sec:ontology}

The CineExplorer ontology provides a conceptual model for representing creative works and the persons involved in them. It is designed to support the annotation of the relational database (Section~\ref{sec:infosystem}) and the construction of the \ac{rdf} knowledge graph deployed in Section~\ref{sec:deployment}.

\subsection{Scope}

The ontology supports three main application directions. It enables actor connectivity analysis through path queries between persons connected via shared creative works. This functionality is enabled by the structure of the \ac{rdf} graph, where persons are linked to works through participation relations.

It also provides a conceptual structure for aligning local entities with external resources such as Wikidata~\cite{wikidata}. In addition, it supports graph-based exploration of related entities through navigation across persons, creative works, genres, and participation relations.

The ontology therefore covers the concepts required for these use cases: \texttt{CreativeWork} instances (\texttt{Film}, \texttt{Series}, and \texttt{Episode}), \texttt{Person}, \texttt{Genre}, descriptive attributes such as language and region, and contextual participation relations. This structure also supports interface-level exploration driven by the knowledge graph.
\subsection{Concept Organization}

The ontology is organized into classes, object properties, and data properties. This separation maintains a clear distinction between entities, relations, and literal attributes.

\subsubsection{Classes}

The central class is \texttt{CreativeWork}, which represents a work independently of its textual representation in the dataset. This abstraction separates the notion of a work from its titles.

\texttt{CreativeWork} is specialized into \texttt{Film}, \texttt{Series}, and \texttt{Episode}. These subclasses are declared mutually disjoint. The class \texttt{Person} represents individuals involved in productions and is specialized into \texttt{Actor}, \texttt{Director}, \texttt{Writer}, \texttt{Editor}, and \texttt{Composer}. These subclasses are not declared disjoint, as multiple roles may be associated with the same individual.

% \paragraph{Relationship to the source data.}
The ontology is informed by the structure of the underlying relational schema. Class and property names correspond to elements present in the dataset, which simplifies the mapping and ensures interpretability. At the same time, the ontology introduces abstraction layers that are not present in the database. In particular, the use of \texttt{CreativeWork} instead of \texttt{Title} and the introduction of \texttt{Participation} reflect conceptual modeling decisions rather than direct schema replication.

% \paragraph{The \texttt{Participation} class.}
The class \texttt{Participation} reifies the relation between a \texttt{Person} and a \texttt{CreativeWork}. This design allows contextual attributes such as role and character name to be associated with the relation itself.

\texttt{Participation} is declared disjoint from \texttt{CreativeWork}, \texttt{Person}, and \texttt{Genre}. Its is conncted to exactly one \texttt{Person} through \texttt{playedBy} and exactly one \texttt{CreativeWork} through \texttt{participatesIn}.

\texttt{Episode} is associated with a \texttt{Series} through \texttt{partOfSeries}.

\subsubsection{Object Properties}

Object properties define relations between entities. These include relations between works and genres, relations between episodes and series, and relations between persons and works through \texttt{Participation}.

The properties \texttt{workedFor} and \texttt{employed} define a general relation between \texttt{Person} and \texttt{CreativeWork}. More specific properties such as \texttt{actedIn}, \texttt{directed}, \texttt{wrote}, \texttt{edited}, and \texttt{composedFor} are defined as sub-properties. Each property is associated with an inverse to support bidirectional navigation.

The property \texttt{workedWith} represents a relation between persons derived from shared participation in the same creative work. It is primarily a traversal-oriented relation and can be derived from participation links, but may also be materialized to simplify querying.

The property \texttt{sameAsExternal} links local resources to external identifiers. No cardinality restriction is imposed on this property.

\subsubsection{Data Properties}

Data properties associate entities with literal values. \texttt{Person} is described by attributes such as name and life dates, plus a multi-valued \texttt{ce:hasProfession} (\texttt{xsd:string}, up to three values per person) that captures the \ac{imdb}-level career-profession axis introduced by the migration. \texttt{CreativeWork} is described by attributes such as title, release year, runtime, content flags, and the popularity composite \texttt{ce:averageRating}~/~\texttt{ce:numVotes} that drives the slice criterion (Section~\ref{sec:dataset}). \texttt{Participation} includes attributes describing roles and character information. \texttt{Episode} includes season and episode numbers.

% \paragraph{Language and region as data properties.}
Language and region are represented as literal attributes of \texttt{CreativeWork}. This choice keeps the ontology simple and consistent with their descriptive role in the dataset.

\subsection{OWL Profile}
\label{subsec:owl-profile}

The ontology targets the \ac{owl}~2 QL profile~\cite{owl2profiles}. Of the four \ac{owl}~2 sub-profiles (\ac{dl}, EL, QL, RL), QL is the one designed for query rewriting over relational databases, with reasoning in $\mathrm{AC}^0$ with respect to the data. This is a natural fit for an \ac{r2rml}-backed deployment where the relational source is the authoritative store and the knowledge graph is large (1.6\,M triples at $N = 5{,}000$).

The constructs actually used by the ontology all fall within QL:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \begin{sloppypar}Subclass and sub-property axioms between named classes and properties (e.g., \texttt{Episode}~$\sqsubseteq$~\texttt{CreativeWork}, \texttt{actedIn}~$\sqsubseteq$~\texttt{workedFor}).\end{sloppypar}
  \item Inverse object properties (\texttt{owl:inverseOf}) for bidirectional navigation.
  \item Symmetric property declarations (\texttt{owl:SymmetricProperty}) for \texttt{ce:workedWith}.
  \item Disjointness axioms over named classes (\texttt{owl:disjointWith}, \texttt{owl:AllDisjointClasses}) for the pairwise disjointness of \texttt{Film}, \texttt{Series}, and \texttt{Episode}, and for the disjointness of \texttt{Person}, \texttt{CreativeWork}, \texttt{Genre}, and \texttt{Participation}.
  \item Domain and range axioms on object and datatype properties.
\end{itemize}

The constructs we deliberately do \emph{not} use are precisely those that would push the ontology out of QL: cardinality restrictions (qualified or unqualified), functional and inverse-functional property declarations, transitive properties, and property chains. Where structural cardinality is genuinely required --- for example the ``exactly one Series per Episode'' invariant or the ``exactly one Person and one CreativeWork per Participation'' constraint --- it is encoded as a \ac{shacl} shape rather than as an \ac{owl} axiom. The reasoning follows the open-world / closed-world distinction: \ac{owl} operates under the open-world assumption, which makes it unsuitable for checking whether a knowledge graph contains all required triples; \ac{shacl} validates the graph against a shapes graph under a closed-world assumption, so an absent required triple is reported as a violation.

Profile membership was confirmed using Protégé's \ac{owl} Profile Checker. We additionally verified consistency using the HermiT reasoner~\cite{hermit}: classification computes all inferred subsumption relations between named classes and checks whether any class is unsatisfiable. No unsatisfiable classes were found, confirming that the ontology is consistent.

\subsection{Namespace and IRIs}

The ontology uses a hash \ac{uri} pattern~\cite{cooluris} with a single namespace: \texttt{http://cineexplorer.local/ontology\#}. Under this pattern, each ontology term is identified by a \ac{uri} fragment (e.g., \texttt{http://cineexplorer.local/ontology\#Film}), and dereferencing any term \ac{iri} resolves to the ontology document. This avoids the HTTP 303 redirect infrastructure required by slash \ac{uri} patterns, making it appropriate for a locally deployed academic project where a publicly accessible server is not available.

\begin{sloppypar}
Instance \acp{iri} follow a separate path-based structure: \texttt{http://\allowbreak cineexplorer.local/\allowbreak data/\{type\}/\{id\}}, reusing the stable \ac{imdb} identifiers from the source dataset as local names. The \ac{iri} design is described further in Section~\ref{sec:mapping}.
\end{sloppypar}

Class and property names follow the naming conventions used throughout the W3C \ac{owl} vocabulary~\cite{owl2primer}: UpperCamelCase for class names (e.g., \texttt{CreativeWork}, \texttt{Participation}) and lowerCamelCase for property names (e.g., \texttt{workedFor}, \texttt{partOfSeries}). All terms carry \texttt{rdfs:label} and \texttt{rdfs:comment} annotations in English.

\subsection{Encountered Problems}

\paragraph{Tooling.}
The ontology was developed in Protégé~5.6.9 using the HermiT reasoner for consistency checking and classification. An early version of the ontology triggered an unsatisfiability due to a conflicting disjointness axiom on \texttt{Participation}; this was resolved by narrowing the disjointness to the intended classes only.

\paragraph{Property directionality.}
\ac{r2rml} generates triples in the direction defined by the mapping template. A property such as \texttt{ce:actedIn} (\texttt{Person} $\to$ \texttt{CreativeWork}) requires a separate triple map for its inverse \texttt{ce:hasActor} if queries need to traverse from work to person. Explicit inverse properties were therefore introduced for each role-specific property to avoid requiring \texttt{OPTIONAL \{ ?w \^{}ce:actedIn ?p \}} patterns in every query.

\paragraph{Participation vs.\ direct properties.}
An initial design used only direct role-specific properties between \texttt{Person} and \texttt{CreativeWork}. The \texttt{Participation} reification was introduced to allow contextual attributes --- character name, job description, credit order --- to be attached to the relation itself rather than to either endpoint. This decision increased the number of triple maps but improved the expressiveness of the model.

\paragraph{Cardinality, the open-world assumption, and the move to SHACL.}
Early drafts encoded structural cardinalities as \ac{owl} axioms: \texttt{owl:qualifiedCardinality "1"} restrictions on \texttt{ce:partOfSeries} (Episode), on \texttt{ce:participatesIn}/\texttt{ce:playedBy} (Participation), and \texttt{owl:maxCardinality "1"} on \texttt{ce:sameAsExternal} (Person, CreativeWork). The functional characteristic was also declared on \texttt{ce:partOfSeries}. These axioms placed the ontology in \ac{owl}~2 \ac{dl}. They were removed in favour of \ac{shacl} shapes for two reasons. First, \ac{owl} cardinalities define class-membership semantics under the open-world assumption rather than data-completeness constraints: an Episode individual with no recorded \texttt{partOfSeries} triple does not violate \texttt{owl:qualifiedCardinality "1"} --- the reasoner simply has no information about its series. What we actually want is a closed-world integrity check over the materialised graph, which is precisely what \ac{shacl} provides. Second, removing these axioms moves the ontology into \ac{owl}~2 QL, opening up query-rewriting deployments and giving AC0 reasoning complexity (Section~\ref{subsec:owl-profile}). The slice-induced invariant that every \texttt{CreativeWork} must carry an \texttt{averageRating} and a \texttt{numVotes} value follows the same pattern and is also encoded as a \ac{shacl} shape.

\paragraph{Two axes for ``role'' vs.\ ``profession''.}
\label{sec:onto-two-axis-discussion}
The migration introduced two related but distinct axes of person classification: per-credit role (the \texttt{role} on each \texttt{Participation}, e.g., this row is an actor credit on this title) and career-level profession (the \ac{imdb} \texttt{primaryProfession}, e.g., this person is identified as a director-and-producer overall). The ontology mirrors this split. \texttt{Participation} carries \texttt{ce:participationRole} as a per-credit string. \texttt{Person} carries \texttt{ce:hasProfession} as the multi-valued career attribute. The \texttt{Person} subclasses (\texttt{Actor}, \texttt{Director}, \texttt{Writer}, \texttt{Editor}, \texttt{Composer}) are populated from \emph{either} axis: an individual is asserted as \texttt{ce:Director} either because they have a director credit on some \texttt{Participation}, or because their \ac{imdb} \texttt{primaryProfession} contains \texttt{director}. Keeping the two axes separate at the data-property level lets queries distinguish ``someone with at least one director credit'' from ``someone \ac{imdb} identifies primarily as a director'', a distinction the previous schema could not express.
