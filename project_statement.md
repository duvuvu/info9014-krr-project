# Knowledge Representation and Reasoning
#### Project outline and guidelines on deliverables

The project’s goal is to **develop an ontology for annotating autonomously developed information systems** and to demonstrate the **meaningful exchange of information** via those annotations. To this end, students form groups of three to develop an information system and a prototype ontology. Collaboration with other groups for the creation of the ontology is allowed. Each group then proceeds with the annotation of their system and demonstrates the meaningful exchange with a **non-trivial demonstrator**.

**(*) Project Roles and Simulation:**
In this project, students will **pretend to be the owner, developer, or maintainer** to simulate a **knowledge graph project**. While knowledge graph projects typically integrate data from various sources, for this 5 ECTS course, the **transformation of a database to a knowledge graph** will suffice. Group collaboration is appreciated and considered for the final grade, particularly if groups share concepts to render the project more realistic. However, **collaboration across groups is optional** for passing the course.

### Milestone 0: Groups
Groups of three students must communicate the following via email:
*   The **names and email addresses** of the group.
*   The **group’s name**.
*   The **chosen domain** and a **description of their information system** (or application).
Students are highly encouraged to draw inspiration from existing applications, provided they cite their sources.

### Milestone 1: Database
The first step is developing the **database underpinning the existing information system**. The goals are to familiarize the group with the domain, refresh modeling skills, and create a **schema and instances simulating a “legacy” information system**. 

**Deliverables for Milestone 1:**
*   A **conceptual or database schema** using a modeling language such as **ERD, UML, or ORM**.
*   An implementation of the relational database provided as an **SQL dump file (DDL statements)**.
*   **Meaningful examples** to populate the database; while a few are acceptable initially, having at least a **couple of dozen examples** makes the exercise more interesting.
*   A **technical report** describing the application and how the conceptual schema and database support the implementation. This report must include **assumptions, integrity constraints, and business rules** that cannot be modeled in the chosen language.

### Milestone 2: Ontology Engineering
To achieve **semantic interoperability**, groups must reach a common agreement on relevant concepts and relations in the form of an **ontology**. Groups may assume an external need to share data with unknown stakeholders and may choose to work together on a common ontology.

**Deliverables for Milestone 2:**
*   One or more **ontologies** featuring a **well-chosen namespace**, all concepts, relations, and ontology individuals.
*   **Human-readable labels and definitions (glosses)** in English.
*   A **technical report** providing information on:
    *   **Design decisions**, such as the choice and motivation for the adopted **OWL 2 profile**.
    *   Motivation for **naming and organizing concepts and instances**.
    *   A description of the **ontology-engineering process**, including tooling, organization (internal and external), and **conflict resolution**.
*   *Optionally*, groups may generate ontology documentation using **WIDOCO**.

**Collaboration Note (*):** If groups work together, they can share the whole ontology or a common core. All involved students must be included as **authors using ontology annotations**. Technical reports are written by each group, but shared paragraphs must be identified with a footnote.

### Milestone 3: Annotating your IS (i.e., creating mappings)
Groups will use the resulting ontology to **annotate their database** using **mappings (with R2RML or RML)** and the resulting **RDF** generated by these mappings.

**Deliverables for Milestone 3:**
*   A **final technical report** describing the development and use of mappings, including **knowledge organization strategies and IRI strategies**.
*   The **mappings** themselves.

### Milestone 4: Using the Ontology and Knowledge Graph
This final milestone consists of three parts:
1.  **Deployment** of the ontology and knowledge graph.
2.  Demonstrating the ability to answer complex questions using **non-trivial SPARQL queries** (including subqueries, federated queries, paths, aggregates, and negation as failure).
3.  A **non-trivial demonstrator**, the scope of which is discussed with the professor. This involves an **applied research study** on topics like big data processing for KGs, research topics in KGs, or the application of class topics like **SHACL or rule languages**.

**Deliverables for Milestone 4 (Technical Report):**
*   Optional revisions of prior chapters.
*   A set of **SPARQL queries** with descriptions.
*   A report on the **non-trivial demonstrator** with references.

**Suggested Structure for the Technical Report:**
*   Titlepage, Abstract, Introduction.
*   Information System (Description and database).
*   Ontology and Ontology Engineering.
*   Mapping Relational Databases to RDF.
*   Deployment and Demonstration (How the KG is used and non-trivial queries).
*   Non-trivial Demonstrator(s).
*   Conclusions, References, and Appendix.

### Presentation
Groups will **present their work and answer questions** during a "defense" held in the semester. This includes a **demo of the knowledge graph and the non-trivial demonstrator**, which can be submitted as a video. Feedback from this session should be used to improve the final report.
